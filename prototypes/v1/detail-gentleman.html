Description

In 1922, Count Alexander Rostov is deemed an unrepentant aristocrat by a Bolshevik tribunal, and is sentenced to house arrest in the Metropol, a grand hotel across the street from the Kremlin.

Rostov, an indomitable man of erudition and wit, has never worked a day in his life, and must now live in an attic room while some of the most tumultuous decades in Russian history unfold outside the window. Unexpectedly, his reduced circumstances provide him entry into a much larger world of emotional discovery.

Review
There is something almost perverse about how pleasant this book is to inhabit. Rostov loses everything — title, fortune, freedom — and becomes the most fully alive person in the room. Towles makes the Metropol a kind of clockwork world: it turns and turns, and inside it Rostov finds a way to make constraint feel like abundance. The pacing is unhurried in exactly the right way.
